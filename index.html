Who We Are

Built by the People
Who Work This Land.

HUNTENGAGE wasn't built in a boardroom. It was built in the field — on timber tracts, creek bottoms, row crop fields, and cutover blocks across the Southeast. Our team includes consulting foresters, wildlife managers, hunters, farmers, land managers, and real estate professionals who saw the leasing process fail everyone involved, year after year.

We recognized that the real problem wasn't finding land or finding hunters. It was the coordination gap — the disconnect between what a landowner expects, what a timber manager needs to protect, and what a hunter wants out of a season. Most platforms hand you a listing and step away. We stay in the conversation.

Our team brings together consulting foresters, wildlife managers, and land professionals who know these properties — their timber rotations, their soil types, their travel corridors. When a planned harvest is on the schedule, we coordinate it. When a food plot opportunity exists, we bring it to the landowner for approval and we handle the work. We make sure hunting activity is compatible with land management plans, that communication flows both ways, and that every season runs without the friction that used to be just accepted as part of the deal.

We saw the friction between hunters and land managers every season. As consulting foresters and land managers ourselves, we built the platform we wished existed.

01
The Land Comes First — Always
Every lease we write is reviewed against the landowner's operation — their timber rotation, their farming schedule, their cattle, their long-term management plan. Hunting is a secondary use of the land, and we structure every agreement to reflect that. When a timber harvest is coming — whether scheduled long in advance or triggered by a fast-moving market — our foresters are already in the conversation with your timber management team. We coordinate as quickly as the situation allows: hunters are notified, access is managed, stands and cameras are relocated where time permits, and the lease continues through the harvest cycle. Most lease platforms can't do this — because most lease platforms don't have consulting foresters on staff who are already part of that conversation.
02
Habitat & Food Plots — Done Right, With Your Approval
For properties in our managed tiers, we actively look for opportunities to improve wildlife habitat — food plot establishment, browse enhancement, edge feathering, mast tree identification. But nothing moves forward without the landowner's explicit approval. Every proposed improvement is presented in writing, reviewed against the property's management plan, and authorized before any work begins. Once approved, our team handles establishment, planting, and ongoing seasonal maintenance. The result is better hunting, higher lease value, and habitat improvements that complement — not conflict with — the land's primary management objectives.

Hunt Management Plan

This plan was put together by an actual hunter and land manager — not generated by AI. It reflects real time on this property: boots on the ground, camera data reviewed, stand sites walked, and harvest history considered.

Season Strategy

This property holds mature timber on the ridges and creek drains, with ag fields on the western boundary that pull deer from neighboring properties during early season. Early archery pressure should be minimized on the ridge stands — save those for late October through the rut. The creek bottom stand on the east side is a high-value rut stand and should not be hunted until mid-October at the earliest.

Wind & Access

Prevailing winds are SW in fall. Access the ridge stands from the north road to avoid blowing scent into the creek bottom. The creek stand requires a W or NW wind — do not hunt it on a S or SE wind. Access from the back gate only.

Turkey Strategy

The hardwood ridges on this property hold turkeys consistently from mid-March through May. Roost trees are concentrated along the east creek drain. Set up 100 yards off the roost on a NW wind, calling softly at first light. The open hardwood flat near the southwest corner is the primary strutting area — glass it from the road before walking in.

Notes

Two longbeards confirmed on camera as of last season. One dominant bird patrols the flat; the second uses the timber edge near the creek. Avoid overcalling. Box call has produced well on this property. Season opens third Saturday in March — confirm current regulations before arrival.

Habitat Improvement Plan

Current habitat is good but can be improved. The following improvements are recommended for landowner approval prior to implementation by the HUNTENGAGE field team.

  • Food plot establishment — 3.2 acres on the SW flat (clover/chicory blend, fall planting)
  • Edge feathering — 400 linear feet along the south fence line
  • Mast tree identification and release — 12 white oaks flagged on the east ridge
  • Browse enhancement — selective clearing in two creek bottom thickets
  • Water source improvement — existing pond needs vegetation management on north bank

All work requires written landowner authorization before any ground disturbance. No chemicals used without explicit approval and documentation.

Wildlife Management Plan Summary

Based on camera inventory and field observation over two seasons. All harvest recommendations are guidelines — lessee makes final determination within legal limits.

  • Antlered deer: Pass all bucks under 3.5 years. Target mature deer 4.5+ only.
  • Antlerless deer: Up to 3 does recommended for herd balance based on current browse pressure.
  • Turkey: 1 longbeard per licensed hunter, no jakes.
  • Hogs: No limit — removal encouraged year-round.
  • Predators: Legal removal encouraged — coyote pressure is significant on this property.
